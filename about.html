About SupplementScore

An evidence-based, non-profit supplement reference. Updated continuously.

What this is

SupplementScore is a public, free reference that grades dietary supplements against published clinical trials. As of May 2026 the database holds 758 supplements across four evidence tiers, documents 1,400+ medication interactions across 70 drug categories with per-molecule detail for the highest-divergence cases, and links every claim back to its original peer-reviewed source.

The site exists because the supplement aisle has grown faster than the evidence base for it. The goal is to make it easy for anyone — without a medical background — to look up whether a supplement is worth taking, who it's worth taking for, and at what dose.

Five principles that drive every editorial decision

The whole site is meant to be auditable, so the principles behind it should be too:

  1. Citation gate over enthusiasm. Tier 1 cannot be reached on mechanistic plausibility, animal data, or industry-funded trials alone. The full rule is in the funder policy.
  2. Funder type is not optional. Every cited study is classified — public, nonprofit, mixed, industry, or undisclosed — and the classification gates the tier call. The four-page funder policy spells out how mixed and undisclosed cases are handled.
  3. Independent second-read. Every pivotal study that moves a tier is read by a second editor without seeing the first reviewer's notes. Disagreements are logged so the reliability of the process can itself be audited over time. See the editorial pipeline.
  4. Score reversals are publicised. Demotions are not quietly rewritten — they are surfaced on the How we changed our mind page along with the triggering evidence. A score that never moves is not a score; it is a static opinion.
  5. No money in the loop. No advertising. No supplement sales. No affiliate links. No free product samples for review. If this changes, this page is updated within seven days and the change appears in the next annual transparency report.

Editorial process — the seven-step pipeline

The shorter version below; the long version with timing and roles is on the editorial pipeline page.

  1. Discovery. Weekly literature scan against PubMed, Cochrane, ClinicalTrials.gov, bioRxiv, EFSA/FDA bulletins.
  2. Triage. Editor classifies each candidate as pivotal, confirmatory, null, retraction-watch, or signal-only.
  3. Full-text review. Pivotal items are read in full and the funder type is set explicitly.
  4. Independent second-read. A second editor reads the same paper without seeing the first extraction.
  5. Score recalculation. The composite is recomputed via the formula e×7 + s×4 + r×3 + o×2 + c×2 + d×2.
  6. Tier-1 gate. Promotions to Tier 1 must satisfy the citation gate; CI checks block deploy if they don't.
  7. Publication + reader feedback loop. Changes ship in the next deploy. Reader inaccuracy reports re-enter at step 3 with a target acknowledgement of 3 business days.

Funding model

SupplementScore is currently self-funded. The site does not accept advertising, does not sell supplements, and does not run affiliate links. If our funding model changes, this section will be updated within seven days of the change taking effect, and the change will be logged in our annual transparency report.

Conflict of interest policy

The editor does not hold equity in any supplement company, does not receive payment from any supplement company, and does not accept free product samples for review. If any contributor or advisor has a financial relationship with a supplement manufacturer, that relationship is disclosed publicly on this page before they review or write anything.

For each cited study, we annotate the funder type (public, nonprofit, mixed, industry, or none_disclosed) and apply a 25% effect-size discount when industry-funded results drive a tier call. See the methodology page for the full rule.

Annual transparency report

Each year we publish a public report covering: how many supplement entries were reviewed, how many tier reassignments were made, how many claims were retracted, how many medication interactions were added, our funding sources, and any conflicts of interest disclosed during the year. The first annual report will publish in January 2027.

Contact

Editorial questions, advisor inquiries, or factual corrections: email the address listed in the main site footer. Inaccuracy reports go through the "Report inaccuracy" button on each supplement card — these are reviewed within 7 days.

Open data

The SupplementScore dataset is published as open data under Creative Commons BY 4.0. Researchers, clinicians, and developers are welcome to download and reuse it with attribution.

  • supplements.json — full database (758 supplements, machine-readable)
  • supplements.csv — flattened for spreadsheets
  • medications.json — 70 medication categories with avoid / caution / depleted-replace lists
  • meds-detail.json — 47 specific drug molecules where intra-class interaction differs meaningfully (statins, SSRIs, DOACs, NSAIDs, beta-blockers, ACE/ARBs, PPIs, anti-epileptics, oncology endocrine, immunosuppressants)
  • pairings.json — 87 supplement-supplement pairings with rationale and dose
  • brands.json — 115 supplements with verified third-party-tested brand entries (NSF, USP, ConsumerLab, LabDoor, IFOS)
  • README.md — schema + citation format
